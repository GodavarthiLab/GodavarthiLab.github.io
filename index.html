Research

A core problem for every synapse

Neurotransmitter phenotype is a dynamically regulated synaptic property — not a fixed feature of neurons.

Every synapse faces the same fundamental challenge: specify the correct transmitter, stabilize that identity across development and plasticity, and modify it when circuits need to adapt or recover. These processes are activity-dependent and operate throughout the life of the animal. And yet the molecular mechanisms that accomplish them remain incompletely understood.

Our lab discovered that postsynaptic neurotransmitter receptors actively regulate presynaptic transmitter identity — retrogradely — through receptor-specific transsynaptic adhesion complexes involving proteins like CASK, Neurexin, Lrp4, and GARLH4. We showed that removing postsynaptic acetylcholine receptors causes loss of presynaptic ACh, and that introducing a non-canonical GABA receptor in muscle is sufficient to drive GABA expression in motor neurons. The synapse is an active, bidirectional negotiation.

We also showed that this negotiation can go wrong: prenatal environmental insults drive aberrant neurotransmitter switching in the developing prefrontal cortex, causing autism-like behavior in adult mice — behavior that can be prevented by restoring the correct transmitter identity early. The same mechanism operates in PTSD and addiction. Understanding the rules, constraints, and leverage points of this system is the central aim of the Godavarthi Lab.

"Understanding when and how synapses choose their language is essential for explaining how brain circuits function, adapt, and fail."

i.
Rules — how do postsynaptic receptors specify and stabilize presynaptic transmitter identity?
Autism Schizophrenia
We have shown that postsynaptic receptors convey their identity to the presynaptic neuron through receptor-specific transsynaptic bridges. In cholinergic synapses, the AChR–Rapsyn–Lrp4–MuSK–Dystroglycan–Neurexin complex stabilizes presynaptic ACh. In GABAergic synapses, a distinct GABAAR–GARLH4–Neuroligin–Neurexin complex achieves the same for GABA. The presynaptic scaffold protein CASK is recruited differentially depending on receptor type, and its nuclear localization suggests a transcriptional decoding role. We are now using proteomics and ChIP-Seq to identify the full interactome of these bridges and the transcriptional programs they activate, in both Xenopus and mouse, to define conserved molecular rules of transmitter specification.
proteomics · ChIP-Seq · Xenopus & mouse NMJ · morpholino knockdown · viral delivery
ii.
Leverage — can receptor engineering reprogram a synapse to restore lost function?
Myasthenia gravis Congenital myasthenic syndrome
In myasthenia gravis, the cholinergic transsynaptic bridge is disrupted — acetylcholine receptors are destroyed by autoimmune attack, and with them the retrograde signal that sustains presynaptic ACh. We demonstrated in Xenopus that this can be bypassed: introducing a non-canonical GABAA receptor in muscle establishes a new GABAergic transsynaptic bridge and restores neuromuscular transmission, rescuing swimming behavior. We are now extending this receptor engineering approach — delivered virally — to mouse models of autoimmune and congenital myasthenia, toward a fundamentally new therapeutic strategy that bypasses immune modulation entirely.
viral gene delivery · mouse NMJ · electrophysiology · behavioral rescue · human iPSC-derived synapses
iii.
Constraints — what makes a neuron susceptible to switching its transmitter identity?
Autism PTSD Addiction
Neurotransmitter switching — where a neuron gains one transmitter and loses another — is how the matching system fails or adapts under pressure. We showed that prenatal immune activation drives GABAergic PFC interneurons to switch to glutamatergic identity, disrupting excitation-inhibition balance and producing autism-like behavior. The same mechanism underlies PTSD and drug-induced cognitive deficits. But only ~12–30% of neurons switch, even under the same stimulus. Why? We are using live-neuron cytoplasmic biopsy and single-cell transcriptomics to identify the molecular state that makes a neuron permissive to switching, with the goal of controlling this process therapeutically.
scRNA-seq · single-cell proteomics · live-neuron biopsy · intersectional mouse genetics · behavioral assays

Dr. Swetha K. Godavarthi
Assistant Professor of Neuroscience
Integrated Sciences Academy, College of Science · Rochester Institute of Technology

Swetha is a cellular and molecular neuroscientist whose work sits at the intersection of synaptic biology, neuroplasticity, and disease. She received her MSc and PhD from the National Brain Research Centre in India, where she studied protein homeostasis in Huntington's disease and Angelman syndrome, before joining Nick Spitzer's lab at UC San Diego — where her interest in neurotransmitter identity took shape.

At UCSD, she made the key discovery that postsynaptic receptors retrogradely stabilize presynaptic neurotransmitter identity through transsynaptic bridges — work published in PNAS. She also demonstrated that prenatal stress drives neurotransmitter switching in the prefrontal cortex, causing autism-like behavior in mice. Most recently she was Research Assistant Professor in Steven Meriney's lab at the University of Pittsburgh, extending this work to mammalian systems.

She is a co-PI on an NSF Standard Grant and a Simons Foundation Empire Faculty Fellow. She has mentored more than a dozen undergraduate researchers and is passionate about building a lab where students from all backgrounds can do rigorous, exciting science.

The Godavarthi Lab will be a place where people openly share where they are stuck, where collective thinking helps catch gaps in reasoning early, and where rigor and warmth coexist.
